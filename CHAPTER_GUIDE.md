# Chapter Writing Guide

*For Stones — A meditation on Kajiwara's "The Direction of Play"*

---

## Structure

Each chapter follows this exact structure:

```
Title
•
"Quote from Kajiwara's book"
•
Meditative reflection
•
Zen koan / contemplation
```

**Four elements. Nothing more.**

---

## Tone & Voice

### The Voice
- **A calm zen teacher** who finds no importance in themselves
- **No explanations** — no "Kajiwara emphasizes..." or "This means..."
- **Direct presence** — describe what IS, not what should be
- **Poetic but simple** — clear, on topic, no flourish for its own sake

### The Style
- **Present tense** — "A stone falls" not "when a stone is placed"
- **Universal perspective** — "we," "you," or just statements of truth
- **Short, breathing phrases** — let silence speak
- **No academic tone** — no explaining, just pointing

---

## The Elements

### 1. Title
The concept being contemplated. Simple, direct.

### 2. Quote
Kajiwara's words from "The Direction of Play"
- Use double quotes: "quote text"
- Let his words stand alone
- No attribution needed — the quote speaks

### 3. Meditative Reflection
**2-4 sentences**, or several very brief statements with space between.

**NOT:** "Kajiwara teaches us that stones have power and we must understand..."
**YES:** "A stone falls to the board. The weight of intention. The permanence of placement."

**Guidelines:**
- Describe what happens, what is
- Short declarative statements
- Use space/line breaks for breathing
- Can be poetic, but never obscure
- Stay on the chapter's topic
- No instructions ("you must" / "one should")

### 4. Zen Koan / Contemplation
A question, paradox, or image to carry forward.

**Forms it can take:**
- A question: "Where does the stone's power come from?"
- A paradox: "The stone is placed. The stone has always been there."
- A contemplation: "What remains when the game is over?"
- An image: "Empty board. Full of possibility."

Not a riddle — something that opens thought rather than closes it.

---

## Example Chapter

```markdown
---
chapter: 1
title: "The Power of Stones"
quote: "Each stone, whether it stands alone or with others, is invested with a power all its own."
---

A stone falls to the board.

The weight of intention. The permanence of placement.

Each stone carries the player within it.

> Before you place the stone, where does its power live?
```

---

## What to Avoid

- ❌ Explaining Kajiwara's ideas
- ❌ Academic or instructional tone
- ❌ "We must" or "one should"
- ❌ Long paragraphs
- ❌ Repeating the quote in different words
- ❌ Koans that are just clever wordplay

---

## What to Embrace

- ✓ Direct, simple truth
- ✓ Space and silence
- ✓ Images from Go (stones, board, placement, breath)
- ✓ Present moment awareness
- ✓ Questions that open rather than close
- ✓ Trust in the reader's contemplation
